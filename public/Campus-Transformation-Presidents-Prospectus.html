You Already Know Something Is Wrong

You may not say it publicly. But you see the numbers.

Enrollment is down. The discount rate keeps climbing. Deferred maintenance is piling up. The endowment — if there is one — is being drawn down to cover operating costs. The board is nervous. Faculty morale is low. And every semester, you wonder if this is the one where the math finally stops working.

You're not alone. American colleges are closing at a rate of two per week. In the last five years, over 80 private nonprofit colleges have closed or merged. Penn State is closing 7 campuses by 2027. The demographic cliff, the AI revolution, and the student loan crisis have together driven public confidence in higher education to historic lows.

The institutions that close are not all small or obscure. They include long-standing regional colleges, faith-based universities, and branch campuses of major systems. The common thread is a business model — tuition-dependent, accreditation-bound, enrollment-driven — that no longer works for a growing number of institutions.

The Question No One Wants to Ask

Most presidents and board chairs are asking: "How do we increase enrollment?"

That may be the wrong question.

The right question might be: "What is the highest and best use of this campus for the community it serves?"

The answer might still be a traditional college. But it might also be something more sustainable, more responsive to the community's actual needs, and more financially viable than chasing a shrinking pool of 18-year-olds.

The cost of waiting is real. Every semester you operate at a loss, your options narrow. Faculty leave. Accreditors issue warnings. The buildings deteriorate. The community loses confidence. By the time most boards act, the only option left is closure and a distressed-price sale to a developer who will tear the buildings down.

There Is a Third Option

The conversation in higher education right now has two options: survive or close. We believe there is a third: transform.

Campus Transformation works with boards of trustees, presidents, and community leaders to explore what a campus could become if it stopped trying to be a traditional college and started becoming what the community actually needs.

That might include:

  • Workforce training center — CDL, welding, healthcare, IT certifications, skilled trades. Employers will pay for training pipelines. Programs like Meta's $115M America's Workforce Academy are actively looking for training sites.
  • Community learning hub — ESL, GED, adult education, senior programming, after-school programs for K-12 students.
  • Business incubator and co-working space — Supporting local entrepreneurs, startups, and remote workers.
  • Event and conference center — Corporate retreats, community events, weddings, civic gatherings.
  • Health and wellness campus — Community health clinic, fitness center, counseling services, wellness programming.
  • Housing — Workforce housing, transitional housing, or community housing using existing dormitory infrastructure.
  • Government services hub — Partnering with local and state government to deliver services from your campus.

None of these require traditional accreditation. All of them generate revenue. Many of them are eligible for federal and state workforce development funding.

What Happens to Your People

This is the question every president asks first. And it should be.

Transformation does not have to mean mass layoffs. Many of your faculty and staff have skills that translate directly into a transformed campus:

  • Faculty become workforce trainers, program coordinators, and curriculum designers
  • Admissions staff become community outreach and enrollment coordinators for new programs
  • Student services staff become case managers and program support
  • Facilities staff continue maintaining the campus
  • Administrative staff support new operations

We work with you to create a staff transition plan that preserves as many jobs as possible. When a campus closes, everyone loses their job. When a campus transforms, many of those jobs can be saved — and new ones created.

If You Are a Board Chair

Your fiduciary responsibility is to the institution and its mission — not to any particular business model. If the traditional model is failing, exploring alternatives is not disloyalty. It is due diligence.

We recommend boards ask their president three questions:

  1. How many semesters of operating runway do we have at current enrollment and discount rates?
  2. What is our plan if enrollment drops another 10-15% over the next two years?
  3. Have we explored any alternatives to either "grow enrollment" or "close"?

If the answers to those questions concern you, a confidential conversation with us costs nothing and commits you to nothing.

If You Are a President

We know this is hard. You took this job because you believe in the mission. You've spent years — maybe decades — serving students and building something meaningful. The idea that it might not survive on your watch is painful.

But the leaders who will be remembered well are the ones who had the courage to act before it was too late. The ones who preserved the campus, preserved jobs, and found a new way to serve the community — rather than handing the keys to a developer and watching the buildings come down.

Transformation is not failure. It is evolution. And it starts with a conversation.
