## Executive Summary

Higher education is facing a convergence of crises that traditional tools cannot solve. Enrollment is declining — the demographic cliff is here, not coming. Retention is eroding, particularly in gateway courses where DFW rates of 20-35% are common and largely accepted as inevitable. Faculty are overwhelmed, teaching larger sections with fewer resources, unable to see which students are struggling until a midterm grade makes it obvious — weeks after the window to intervene has closed.

The gradebook, the primary instrument of student assessment for over a century, tells faculty one thing: what happened. It cannot tell them what is happening right now, what is likely to happen next, or what specific intervention would change the trajectory. A student with a C- in Biology could be struggling with cellular respiration, or with scientific reasoning, or with exam anxiety, or with all three. The grade collapses all of that complexity into a single letter that arrives too late to act on.

AI is transforming every industry except the one that matters most. Education technology has spent two decades digitizing the same broken processes — putting gradebooks online, automating multiple choice, building adaptive quiz engines that are really just branching logic with better fonts. None of it has changed the fundamental problem: faculty cannot see what students actually understand.

Research supports a different approach. Decades of work in formative assessment (Black & Wiliam, 1998), deliberate practice (Ericsson, 2006), and self-regulated learning (Zimmerman, 2002) demonstrate that learning improves when students receive continuous, specific feedback about their understanding — not periodic grades that summarize the past. Adaptive learning research (Corbett & Anderson, 1995; VanLehn, 2011) shows that AI-driven tutoring can approach the effectiveness of human one-on-one instruction. And work on learning analytics (Siemens, 2013) establishes that student trajectory data, when available in real time, enables interventions that measurably improve outcomes.

What has been missing is a system that combines all of these insights into a single, governed, faculty-friendly platform. One that maps skills from any syllabus, tracks proficiency continuously across multiple evidence sources, calculates confidence in its own assessments, detects when trajectories are declining, distinguishes genuine learning from noise, and coaches students through their specific gaps — all while enforcing fairness, transparency, and human oversight at every decision point.

That is what Transform Learning built.

The platform is live. Students can sign up free and upload any syllabus, program guide, or course list. Faculty can run structured 90-day pilots. The AI coaching system is ready for paid subscribers. A parallel institutional implementation is in development for B2B sales.

The founding team brings 30+ years of classroom experience, enterprise technology leadership, and sales expertise. The market is $76 billion in US higher education technology spending, growing as institutions face existential pressure to improve retention and demonstrate learning outcomes for accreditation.

The question is not whether AI will transform how students learn. The question is whether the tools that do it will be built by people who understand education, or by people who understand software. Transform Learning was built by educators first.

## Intellectual Property

**Provisional patents (draft, not yet filed):**

1. **Learning Genuineness Score** — A continuous metric (0-1) that distinguishes real learning from lucky guesses using directional consistency, exponentially-weighted velocity, and adaptive anomaly detection
2. **Governed AI Proficiency Pipeline** — A four-layer governance system (input validation → score audit → uncertainty estimation → decision governance) with hierarchical deferral to human review
3. **Multi-Source Proficiency Estimation** — Source-normalized evidence weighting with trajectory-aware adjustment and governed output

**Action required:** File all three provisionals immediately. The methodology was first publicly accessible approximately April 4, 2026. Under US patent law, we have one year from public disclosure to file. Cost estimate: $6,000-$15,000 for all three provisionals.

**Trade secrets:** The specific prompt engineering, scoring weights, governance thresholds, and coaching pace system are not disclosed in the patents and constitute trade secrets. The two implementations should be compared to determine if they produce meaningfully different algorithmic results, which could strengthen or expand IP claims.

**Trademark:** "Transform Learning" should be registered. Cost: ~$350 per class via USPTO.

**Brand & Domain:** The original product name "Arrival" (arrival.ai) is taken as a domain. Research is ongoing into alternative product names and available domains. The company operates under "Transform Learning" at transformlearning.ai. Current tagline: "Changing how learning works."

**Note on patents as strategy:** Patents in ed-tech rarely provide strong competitive moats on their own. Speed of adoption, published research, and institutional relationships are stronger defensibility. File the provisionals to establish priority, but do not treat patents as the core strategy.

## Revenue Model

### B2C: Students (Now)

| Tier | Price | What they get |
|------|-------|--------------|
| Free | $0 | Upload syllabus/program, skill mapping, proficiency tracking, quizzes, study guides, peer learning |
| Coach | $10/month | Everything free + unlimited AI coaching conversations with pace control |

**Why free matters:** Every free student is a data point, a testimonial source, and a future institutional sale. When a student shows their professor the skill map, the professor asks "where did you get this?" That's a B2B lead generated by a B2C user.

**Honest assessment:** B2C alone will not sustain the company. Conversion rates for student tools are typically 1-3%. The coaching paywall is a bridge to sustainability and a signal of product value, not the long-term business. The real revenue is B2B.

### B2B: Institutions (Starting Fall 2026)

**Pricing (value-anchored):**

| Tier | Price |
|------|-------|
| Course License | $1,000/course/semester |
| Department Bundle | $10,000/year |
| Institutional License | $40,000/year |

**Value framing:** If Transform Learning retains 5 additional students in a gateway course, at $8,000 average tuition: 5 × $8,000 = $40,000. The institution breaks even on an annual license immediately.

**Who buys:** The budget owner varies by institution — could be the Provost, a Dean, a Department Chair with discretionary funds, or a Student Success office. The sales process must identify the right buyer at each institution.

## Go-to-Market Strategy

Transform Learning uses a **Faculty-Led Bottom-Up Adoption Model.** We do not sell to institutions first. We create demand inside courses.

**Phase 1** — Faculty pilot entry
**Phase 2** — Course success with evidence
**Phase 3** — Department expansion
**Phase 4** — Institutional license

This mirrors successful adoption paths used by Canvas, Gradescope, and Perusall.

### Ideal First Customers

**Primary:** Small-mid private colleges with enrollment pressure, high DFW gateway courses, flexible decision-making, and teaching-focused faculty.

**Secondary:** Regional publics, community colleges, online programs.

**First course targets:** Intro Biology, College Algebra, Intro Psychology, Writing I, Chemistry I — these have the highest DFW rates nationally.

| Week | What happens |

Pilots have a deadline. At day 90, the conversation shifts to: "Here's what happened. Here's what a course license costs."

This is a bottom-up sales motion. Every stage produces evidence for the next.

## Research Strategy

Transform Learning's credibility depends on evidence, not promises. Research is not optional — it is the sales strategy.

**Study 1: Effectiveness** — Partner with 3-5 faculty to run comparisons. Students using Transform Learning vs. students without it. Measure: DFW rates, proficiency improvement, engagement, satisfaction. Begin during summer pilots — do not wait until fall.

**Study 2: UI/UX** — How do students interact with AI coaching? What works? What frustrates them? How does pace control affect outcomes? Partner with a faculty member in education or HCI.

**Study 3: AI Coaching Effectiveness** — Free tier users vs. coaching subscribers. Does coaching make a measurable difference? This data justifies the $10/month price and the institutional coaching tier.

**Publication targets:** Journal of Educational Technology, EDUCAUSE Review, AERA, OLC. Even a working paper on our website adds credibility.

**Research partnerships:** Faculty who run pilots can co-author papers. Publication incentive for them, third-party validation for us.

**Critical note:** Even imperfect, quasi-experimental data from the first pilots is more valuable than a perfectly designed study that starts in fall. Get data fast.
